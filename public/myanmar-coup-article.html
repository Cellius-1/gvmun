Four Years On: How the Coup Has Shaped Myanmar

Recent Developments and Their Impact

Lisha Pitso

Winter 2025 Journalism Intern

February 1, 2025
10 min read

Myanmar four years after the military coup of February 1, 2021. (File photo)

Four years after the military took control of Myanmar on February 1, 2021, the nation is still enmeshed in a severe humanitarian catastrophe, a collapsing economy, and widespread violence. Aung San Suu Kyi's democratically elected government was overthrown in a coup, sparking a protracted civil conflict that would have a significant impact on the nation's future.

Intensification of Military Hostilities

After the coup, countrywide protests erupted demanding the return of civilian rule. The junta's brutal crackdown on these protests led to the emergence of armed resistance factions, in particular the People's Defence Force (PDF), formed by the National Unity Government (NUG), a shadow government comprised of ousted legislators. These factions have joined forces with established ethnic armed groups, escalating the conflict.

Recent reports show that the junta governs merely 21% of Myanmar's territory, struggling against PDF units and ethnic armed organizations. The armed forces have endured major losses, encompassing crucial trade routes and military installations. Internal conflicts within the military have emerged as well, with demands for the ousting of its leader, Min Aung Hlaing.

Humanitarian and Financial Crisis

The ongoing violence has led to a serious humanitarian crisis. Over 4 million people have been displaced, and half of the population is now impoverished. Access to essential utilities has deteriorated; less than 50% of people can now access power. The United Nations has warned about an oncoming possibility of catastrophic hunger in Rakhine State, mainly hurting the Rohingya Muslim community.

Myanmar's economy is in ruins. Widespread inflation has resulted from the kyat's sharp decline and the country's GDP shrinking significantly. Many young people have fled the country or joined rebel groups as a result of the military's implementation of mandatory conscription, which has put more strain on the labor market.

Crucial infrastructure, such as energy facilities, has come under the control of resistance groups, intensifying the junta's difficulties.

Global and Domestic Forces

The junta is facing increased isolation on a global scale. The International Criminal Court has issued an arrest warrant for Min Aung Hlaing for crimes against humanity connected to the abuse of the Rohingya. China has been crucial in the area; while Beijing first backed the junta, it has since pushed for elections, which are generally seen as a front.

In Myanmar, the military persists in using savage methods, such as sexual violence and mass executions, to uphold authority, especially in large urban areas. In spite of these efforts, resistance groups have achieved notable successes, seizing towns and key locations. The civil war has led to more than 6,000 civilian fatalities and the uprooting of close to 3.5 million individuals.

Outlook for the Future

With the ongoing conflict, the potential for the junta's downfall is being thought about more frequently. Nonetheless, even if the military government collapses, difficulties will persist. The NUG and supporting resistance groups must manage the challenges of unifying various ethnic factions and creating a stable, democratic governance system. The role of the international community will be vital in aiding a shift towards a federal democratic system that meets the hopes of Myanmar's varied population.

In summary, four years following the coup, Myanmar finds itself at a pivotal point. The remarkable resilience and defiance of its citizens in the face of military oppression have been significant. However, the journey toward peace and democracy is still filled with obstacles that will need ongoing internal determination and global assistance to address.
