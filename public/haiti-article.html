Haiti's Crisis: A Policy Analysis

GVMUN Research Team

Policy Analysis Division

March 15, 2025
15 min read

Haiti faces ongoing political and humanitarian challenges. (File photo)

Haiti continues to face unprecedented challenges as political instability, economic collapse, and humanitarian crises converge to create one of the most complex situations in the Western Hemisphere. This analysis examines the multifaceted nature of Haiti's crisis and explores potential policy solutions for sustainable development and stability.

Historical Context and Root Causes

Haiti's current crisis cannot be understood without examining its historical foundations. The country has faced persistent challenges since its independence in 1804, including colonial legacy effects, international debt burdens, and repeated cycles of political instability. The 2010 earthquake further devastated an already fragile infrastructure, while the 2021 assassination of President Jovenel Mo√Øse created a significant power vacuum.

Structural issues including weak governance institutions, limited economic opportunities, and environmental degradation have created a complex web of challenges that require comprehensive, long-term solutions rather than short-term interventions.

Current Security Challenges

The security situation in Haiti has deteriorated dramatically, with armed gangs controlling an estimated 60% of Port-au-Prince and surrounding areas. These groups have disrupted essential services, blocked fuel supplies, and created a climate of fear that has paralyzed normal economic and social activities.

The humanitarian impact has been severe, with thousands of people displaced from their homes and limited access to basic services including healthcare, education, and clean water. The situation has been compounded by a cholera outbreak and ongoing food insecurity affecting millions of Haitians.

Economic Implications

Haiti's economy has contracted significantly, with GDP declining and inflation reaching critical levels. The informal economy, which employs the majority of Haitians, has been particularly affected by the security crisis. International trade has been disrupted, affecting both imports of essential goods and exports of agricultural products.

Remittances from the Haitian diaspora remain a crucial source of foreign currency, but the deteriorating security situation has made it increasingly difficult for families to access these funds safely.

International Response and Policy Options

The international community has responded with various initiatives, including humanitarian aid, diplomatic efforts, and discussions about security intervention. However, the effectiveness of these responses has been limited by the complexity of the situation and the need for Haitian-led solutions.

Policy recommendations include strengthening local governance institutions, supporting community-based security initiatives, investing in economic development programs, and addressing the root causes of instability through comprehensive reforms.

Path Forward

Sustainable solutions for Haiti require a coordinated approach that addresses immediate security concerns while building long-term institutional capacity. This includes supporting democratic governance, promoting economic diversification, investing in education and healthcare, and addressing environmental challenges.

The role of the Haitian diaspora and regional partners will be crucial in developing and implementing effective policies that respect Haitian sovereignty while providing necessary support for stability and development.

    Situation in Haiti: UNICEF Warns About Surge in Youth Armed Recruitment

    Highlight on the Worsening Humanitarian Crisis

    Prisha Aurora
    January 18, 2025
    6 min read
    892 views

    Prisha Aurora

    Winter 2025 Journalism Intern at GVMUN

    prisha.aurora@gvmun.org

    Haiti is facing a worsening humanitarian crisis, with a dramatic rise in the recruitment of children into armed gangs. According to a recent report by UNICEF, the number of children being forcibly or coercively recruited by criminal groups has surged by 70% over the past year.

    Critical Statistics: It is estimated that children now make up between 30% to 50% of gang members in Haiti, a troubling statistic that underscores the increasing vulnerability of minors in the country's deteriorating security landscape.

    70% Increase in Child Recruitment
    50% Max Gang Composition (Children)
    85% Port-au-Prince Under Gang Control
    700K Displaced People

    Root Causes of Child Recruitment

    The recruitment of children into armed groups is fueled by extreme poverty, political instability, and the near-total collapse of state institutions. Gangs, which now control approximately 85% of the capital, Port-au-Prince, have taken advantage of the absence of government oversight and law enforcement to expand their influence.

    Vulnerable Demographics

    Children as young as eight are being abducted, forcibly recruited, or lured with promises of food, protection, and money.

    Poverty Statistics

    More than 60% of the population lives on less than $4 a day, making children particularly vulnerable to exploitation.

    The Reality for Haiti's Children

    For boys, the most common roles within these gangs include acting as lookouts, informants, and active combatants. Many are armed and forced to participate in violent acts, including kidnappings, assassinations, and street battles between rival gangs. The psychological and emotional toll on these children is devastating, as they are often exposed to extreme brutality and trauma at an early age.

    For girls, the situation is even more dire. Many are subjected to sexual exploitation, forced domestic labor, and other forms of abuse at the hands of gang members. Reports indicate that rape and other forms of gender-based violence are being systematically used as tools of control and intimidation.

    International Response and Funding Needs

    UNICEF and other humanitarian organizations are calling for urgent intervention to address the crisis. The agency has requested $38 million in emergency funding to provide educational programs aimed at preventing child recruitment. UNICEF emphasizes that education is one of the most effective tools in keeping children away from gangs, offering them an alternative to a life of violence.

    Key Challenges
    • Funding shortages limiting aid delivery and educational services
    • Ongoing security crisis making humanitarian access difficult
    • Need for stronger child protection and rehabilitation programs
    • Requirement for increased law enforcement to dismantle criminal networks
