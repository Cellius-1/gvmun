Discussing the Recent US Ban on TikTok

TikTok and the New Frontline in the US China Tech War

Angela Li
January 21, 2025
7 min read
542 views

Angela Li

Winter 2025 Journalism Intern at GVMUN

angela.li@gvmun.org

In recent years, the global technological landscape has been reshaped by geopolitical tensions between the United States and China, with one of the most recent high-profile battles taking place over TikTok. The US government's increasing scrutiny of TikTok has led to a ban of TikTok on January 19th, 2025 due to national security concerns over data privacy and potential ties to the Chinese government.

Key Development: As of January 20th, 2025, on the same day of President Trump's inauguration and beginning of his second term in office, TikTok is back online for American Users. President Trump plans to restore TikTok by delaying the ban for 90 more days to work out "a 50-50 joint venture between TikTok's existing parent company, China-based ByteDance, and a new, American owner".

Not only do most American citizens oppose the ban, but in this period of uncertainty, a portion have fled to alternative platforms, mainly the Chinese social media app Xiaohongshu (Rednote). This shift has significant ramifications for both China's online influence and the ongoing geopolitical contest between the two superpowers.

The Battle for Digital Dominance

The potential US ban on TikTok is much more than a matter of data privacy. It is part of a larger geopolitical struggle between the US and China, where influence over digital platforms has become a strategic tool in shaping global narratives. The Biden administration had made two national security arguments about TikTok:

Security Concerns

Fear that China could access users' information as potential blackmail material, raising concerns about national security and privacy.

Content Manipulation

Concerns that the company could manipulate content in a way that benefits the Chinese government's interests and narratives.

President Biden thus signed a law last April that gave ByteDance 270 days to sell TikTok to an owner from the United States or one of its allies or face a ban. As US users begin to migrate to Chinese platforms due to the ban on TikTok, they are not just engaging in rare cultural exchange with Chinese social media users over the internet, but also entering an ecosystem that offers a distinct perspective on global trends and ideologies.

Implications for US-China Relations

The rising popularity of Xiaohongshu and other Chinese platforms is part of a broader strategy by China to expand its digital influence and reshape the global narrative. Though Xiaohongshu is scrambling to find ways to moderate English-language content according to Chinese censorship requirements and build English-Chinese translation tools, Chinese state media has overall cheered the trend.

Media Response: State broadcaster CCTV said that the TikTok users had found a "new home," highlighting how this development aligns with China's broader digital diplomacy objectives.

This shift could affect how countries view China's role in the digital economy, and the influence of Chinese tech companies could challenge the dominance of Western platforms like Facebook, Instagram, and Twitter, particularly in markets outside of the US and Europe. The US is evidently wary of China's growing influence and is increasingly focused on controlling Chinese media's reach as a critical aspect of the broader geopolitical battle for media dominance.

Looking Forward: The Digital Cold War

As TikTok refugees flood to platforms like Xiaohongshu, they are not just finding new spaces for entertainment, but also contributing to a broader geopolitical shift in a way that aligns with the strategic interests of the US and China. The ongoing TikTok saga represents more than just a battle over a single app—it symbolizes the complex intersection of technology, national security, and global influence in the digital age.

Key Takeaways
  • The TikTok ban represents a new front in US-China technological competition
  • User migration to Chinese platforms may inadvertently boost China's digital influence
  • The resolution will set precedents for future digital sovereignty debates
  • Both nations are using digital platforms as tools of soft power and narrative control
